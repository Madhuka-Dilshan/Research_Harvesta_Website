Smart Tomato Farming

This research develops a smart greenhouse system for tomato cultivation, integrating image processing, machine learning, and environmental sensing to enhance ripeness detection, disease identification, and resource management. By enabling precise irrigation, fertilization, and pest control, the system aims to boost sustainability, reduce waste, and improve tomato yield and quality . The solution emphasizes real-time monitoring and decision-making, reducing human error and dependence on manual labor. It ultimately supports efficient, eco-friendly tomato farming through data-driven automation, contributing to food security and agricultural innovation. Furthermore, the system leverages Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and ensemble models for high-accuracy classification of tomato ripeness stages and early detection of plant diseases, allowing timely intervention. Environmental sensors track key parameters such as temperature, humidity, soil moisture, and nutrient levels, ensuring optimized resource use. IoT integration facilitates continuous data collection and remote control, enabling farmers to make proactive decisions. The model also incorporates predictive analytics to forecast crop needs based on plant age and environmental trends. This end-to-end intelligent system not only enhances productivity and quality but also lowers input costs and environmental impact. The modular design allows scalability to other crops, making it a versatile framework for precision agriculture. By integrating multiple technologies into a unified platform, the research addresses critical gaps in conventional farming and paves the way for fully automated, smart greenhouse operations.

LITERATURE SURVEY

Advancements in artificial intelligence, particularly deep learning, have shown promising results in addressing key challenges in agriculture. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have been widely adopted for plant disease detection and severity classification. Studies such as Mohanty et al. (2016) demonstrated the capability of CNNs to accurately classify plant diseases using leaf images. However, differentiating between closely related severity stages (e.g., mild vs. moderate infections) continues to present challenges, requiring robust labeled datasets and data augmentation techniques for improved accuracy. For pest identification, the Xception model and other deep learning architectures have shown high precision in image-based pest detection (Ferentinos, 2018). Mobile-integrated pest detection systems have improved accessibility for farmers, but model adaptability across varying lighting and environmental conditions remains an active area of research. In the domain of irrigation and fertilization, IoT-based sensor networks combined with ensemble learning models like Random Forest and XGBoost have improved prediction accuracy (Patel et al., 2020). These models consider dynamic variables such as soil nutrients, moisture, and crop age, offering efficient and sustainable solutions. Ripeness detection using deep learning (e.g., YOLO, Faster R-CNN) enables real-time fruit monitoring, supporting optimal harvest scheduling (Kamilaris & Prenafeta-Boldú, 2018). However, integrating these models into a single, scalable, and real-time agricultural decision support system remains a research gap that this study aims to address.

METHODOLOGY


This research adopts a machine learning-driven methodology to enhance precision agriculture through disease detection, pest identification, ripeness sensing, and irrigation and fertilization optimization. Tomato disease severity was classified using the EfficientNet-B0 model, trained with augmented image data labeled under expert supervision. Ripeness stages were detected using YOLOv8 and Faster R-CNN, with preprocessed greenhouse images enhancing model accuracy. Pest identification employed a pre-trained Xception model, utilizing image augmentation and a custom classifier for robust multi-class classification. Irrigation and fertilization predictions were based on sensor data, with preprocessing techniques such as normalization, anomaly detection, and encoding applied. Ensemble models like RandomForestRegressor, XGBRegressor, CatBoostRegressor, and LGBMRegressor were used to enhance predictive accuracy. Performance was evaluated using MAE, MSE, RMSE, and R² metrics. The integration of these models enables real-time monitoring, precise environmental recommendations, and decision-making support, ultimately minimizing resource wastage and improving crop health and yield across different stages of cultivation.

RESEARCH PROBLEM

Precision agriculture requires timely and accurate decision-making to optimize resource usage and improve crop productivity. However, traditional methods for detecting plant diseases, monitoring pests, determining fertilizer and water requirements, and assessing fruit ripeness are often labor-intensive, time-consuming, and inconsistent. This research addresses the problem of integrating deep learning and ensemble machine learning models to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of agricultural decision-making processes. Specifically, the study explores how deep learning can improve disease detection and severity classification in tomato crops, enabling faster and more cost-effective interventions. It also investigates the role of real-time pest monitoring using machine learning to minimize pesticide use and improve greenhouse pest control. Furthermore, the study examines how environmental factors and plant age influence irrigation and fertilization, and how ensemble models can improve predictive accuracy. Finally, it evaluates the use of deep learning models for fruit ripeness detection to optimize harvest timing, reduce post-harvest losses, and improve overall crop yield.

Main Objective

Achieve sustainable greenhouse management through smart technology integration, using IoT sensors, image processing for predictive adjustments, and user-friendly interfaces to enhance agricultural efficiency and sustainability.


Sub Objective 1

Investigate deep learning models like YOLOv8 in automating fruit ripeness detection and optimizing harvest timing.


Sub Objective 2

Analyze how environmental variables and plant age affect fertilizer and water predictions using ensemble models.


Sub Objective 3

Assess real-time pest detection with machine learning's impact on reducing pesticide use in greenhouses.


Sub Objective 4

Evaluate deep learning models' effectiveness in detecting and classifying tomato disease severity compared to traditional methods.

RESEARCH GAP

Despite advancements in precision agriculture, significant research gaps remain in the integration and practical deployment of machine learning and deep learning models across diverse agricultural scenarios. Current studies often focus on isolated applications such as disease detection, pest control, or irrigation prediction, lacking a unified system that addresses all critical aspects of crop management. Many existing models are trained on limited or non-diverse datasets, reducing their generalizability across different environmental conditions and crop varieties. Furthermore, real-time deployment and user-friendly interfaces for farmers remain underdeveloped, limiting accessibility and practical use. Most traditional models fail to consider dynamic factors such as plant age, weather variability, and nutrient interactions, which are crucial for accurate fertilizer and water predictions. Additionally, while fruit ripeness detection models exist, they often lack the precision needed for optimal harvest timing and waste reduction. This research aims to bridge these gaps by developing a comprehensive, real-time, and adaptive system using robust deep learning and ensemble models.
